What to know about Sourdough

Sourdough is a type of bread made from naturally fermented dough. It requires only flour, water, and salt, along with wild yeast and bacteria present in the environment. Sourdough relies on wild yeast and lactobacilli bacteria present in the environment. These microorganisms are captured in a starter culture, which is a mixture of flour and water that's regularly fed to keep it active and healthy. Remember sourdough is living and requires care, think of it as a pet.

Things to remember about Sourdough

Key Things:

  1. Natural Fermentation: Sourdough relies on wild yeast and bacteria for leavening, captured in a starter culture of flour and water.
  2. Complex Flavor: The fermentation process gives sourdough its distinct tangy flavor and creates a more complex taste profile compared to bread made with commercial yeast.
  3. Potential Health Benefits: Sourdough fermentation breaks down gluten and phytic acid, making it easier to digest and potentially offering a lower glycemic index compared to other bread types.

Supplies you will need

Keep in mind these are basic supplies for baking and for handling your sourdough. More can be added to your collection as you grow to master the art of baking sourdough.

  • Flour: High-quality flour is essential for sourdough baking. All-purpose flour or bread flour works well, but you can also experiment with whole wheat or rye flour for added flavor.
  • Water: Use filtered or non-chlorinated water for feeding your starter and mixing your dough.
  • Container: A jar or container to store your sourdough starter. Choose a container with enough room for your starter to expand during fermentation.
  • Kitchen Scale: A digital kitchen scale is useful for accurately measuring ingredients by weight, which is important for consistent results in sourdough baking.
  • Mixing Bowls: You'll need mixing bowls of various sizes for mixing and fermenting your dough.
  • Dough Scraper: A dough scraper is handy for handling and shaping sticky sourdough dough.
  • Knife: Also known as a bench scraper, this tool helps with dividing and shaping dough.
  • Banneton or Proofing Basket: A banneton or proofing basket is used to support the shape of your dough during the final proofing stage.
  • Linen Cloth or Kitchen Towel: Use a linen cloth or kitchen towel to line your proofing basket and cover your dough during fermentation.
  • Dutch Oven or Baking Stone: A Dutch oven or baking stone creates steam in the oven, resulting in a crispy crust for your sourdough bread.
  • Lame or Sharp Knife: A lame (pronounced lah-may) or sharp knife is used to score the top of your bread before baking, allowing it to expand properly in the oven.
  • Oven Thermometer: An oven thermometer ensures that your oven is at the correct temperature for baking.
  • Cooling Rack: A cooling rack allows air to circulate around your freshly baked bread, preventing it from becoming soggy.

Getting a Starter

Creating a starter

Creating a sourdough starter from scratch is a rewarding process that requires just two simple ingredients: flour and water. Here's a basic guide to creating your own sourdough starter:

Ingredients:

  • Whole grain flour (such as whole wheat or rye flour)
  • All-purpose flour (or bread flour)
  • Water (non-chlorinated, preferably filtered)
  1. Day 1: In a clean jar or container, mix together 50 grams (or about 1/4 cup) of whole grain flour with 50 grams (or about 1/4 cup) of water until well combined. Make sure the consistency is similar to thick pancake batter. Cover the jar loosely with a lid or cloth and let it sit at room temperature (ideally around 70-75째F or 21-24째C) for 24 hours.
  2. Day 2: After 24 hours, discard half of the mixture in the jar. Add to the jar 50 grams (or about 1/4 cup) of whole grain flour and 50 grams (or about 1/4 cup) of water. Stir until well combined. Cover the jar loosely and let it sit at room temperature for another 24 hours.
  3. Day 3 and beyond: 6. Repeat the discarding and feeding process once a day. As the starter becomes more active, you may notice bubbles forming on the surface and a slightly sour smell. Continue daily feedings until your starter is consistently doubling in size within 6-8 hours after feeding and has a pleasant sour aroma. This process typically takes 5-7 days but may vary depending on environmental factors.

Aquiring a pre-started starter

  1. Review Instructions: If you received a pre-started sourdough starter from a friend or purchased one, review any instructions provided. Different starters may have specific care requirements, such as feeding ratios or storage conditions.
  2. Transfer and Assess: Transfer the pre-started starter to a clean jar or container if it's not already in one. Take a moment to observe its appearance and smell. A healthy starter should have a slightly sour smell and show signs of activity, such as bubbles on the surface.
  3. Feed the Starter: If the pre-started starter hasn't been recently fed, it's a good idea to give it a feeding to refresh it. Follow the feeding instructions provided with the starter or use a 1:1:1 ratio (equal parts by weight or volume of starter, flour, and water). Discard a portion of the starter before feeding to maintain the right ratio and prevent overflow.
  4. Observe and Repeat: After feeding, observe the starter over the next several hours. It should become active, showing signs of fermentation. Depending on its condition and your baking schedule, you may need to repeat the feeding process daily or every few days.
  5. Consistency: Consistency is key to caring for your pre-started sourdough starter. Stick to a regular feeding schedule and observe how the starter responds to feeding and ambient conditions. Adjust your feeding frequency or ratios as needed to keep the starter healthy and active.
  6. Store Properly: When not in use, store your pre-started sourdough starter in the refrigerator. This slows down fermentation and reduces the need for frequent feedings. Before using the starter in a recipe, bring it to room temperature and give it a feeding to refresh it.

All sourdough must be fed depending on usage.

Feeding the Starter

It is essential to keep your sourdough starter healthy and active. Here's how to do it:

  • Preparation: Before feeding your starter, ensure your hands and utensils are clean, and prepare a clean jar or container to hold your starter.
  • Start by discarding a portion of your starter. You can discard anywhere from a small portion to half of the starter, depending on your preference and how much starter you want to maintain.
  • Flour and Water: After discarding, replenish the remaining starter with equal parts of flour and water. A common ratio is 1:1:1, meaning equal parts by weight or volume of starter, flour, and water. For example, if you have 100 grams of starter, you would add 100 grams of flour and 100 grams of water.
  • Mix: Stir the mixture thoroughly until the flour and water are fully incorporated into the starter. Ensure there are no dry pockets of flour.
  • Cover and Let Rest: Cover the jar or container loosely with a lid or cloth. Let the starter rest at room temperature, ideally between 70-75째F (21-24째C), away from drafts and direct sunlight.
  • Observation: After feeding, observe your starter over the next several hours. It should become active, showing signs of fermentation such as bubbles forming on the surface and a slight increase in volume.
  • Maintenance: Depending on your baking schedule and the activity of your starter, you may need to repeat the feeding process daily or every few days to keep it healthy and active.
  • Remember to maintain consistency in feeding your starter. Regular feedings and observation will help you maintain a strong and active sourdough starter, ready to leaven your bread.
